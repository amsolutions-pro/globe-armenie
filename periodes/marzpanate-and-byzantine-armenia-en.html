Marzpanate and Byzantine Armenia

428 – 640
Partition: Sasanian Persia (east) · Byzantine Empire (west)

In the east, Sasanian Persia governed Persarmenia through a marzpan (viceroy) seated at Dvin; in the west, Emperor Justinian I reorganised his Armenian provinces (Armenia I–IV) in 536 through Novel 31, replacing the nakharars' hereditary authority with Roman law and justifying the change by calling local inheritance customs "barbaric" — an Armenian revolt broke out as early as 538-539 in response. The Byzantine general Narses, of Armenian origin, served Justinian until his defeat of the Ostrogoths in Italy (552).

In 451, the Persian attempt to impose Zoroastrianism provoked the battle of Avarayr (26 May): the sparapet Vardan Mamikonian, the kingdom's supreme military commander, died in battle against a larger Sasanian army, according to Armenian sources — a military defeat, but a moral victory that the Church canonised (the feast of Vartanank'). A generation later, his nephew Vahan Mamikonian took up arms again in the 480s: his revolt led to the treaty of Nvarsak (484), which finally guaranteed the religious freedom Vardan had died for.

In 591, Byzantine emperor Maurice restored the young Chosroes II, driven out by a usurper, to the Persian throne, in exchange for the frontier moving east in Byzantium's favour. This same Chosroes II took revenge on his benefactor: in 602 Emperor Maurice was overthrown and executed by the usurper Phocas — Chosroes II invoked this murder as a casus belli, took the Byzantine fortress of Dara after a long siege in 604, and recaptured Dvin, barely restored in 591: Armenia then served as a battlefield and logistical base for Emperor Heraclius's campaigns across the Caucasus (624-625). The peace of 628, after Chosroes II's fall, brought Armenia back under Byzantine influence — a fragile, short-lived reunification, swept away by 640 with the first Arab raids.
