Marzpanate and Byzantine Armenia

428 – 640
Partition: Sasanian Persia (east) · Byzantine Empire (west)

In the east, Sasanian Persia governed Persarmenia through a marzpan (viceroy) seated at Dvin; in the west, Emperor Justinian I reorganised his Armenian provinces (Armenia I–IV) in 536 through Novel 31, replacing the nakharars' hereditary authority with Roman law and justifying the change by calling local inheritance customs "barbaric" — an Armenian revolt broke out as early as 538-539 in response. The Byzantine general Narses, of Armenian origin, served Justinian until his defeat of the Ostrogoths in Italy (552).

In 451, the Persian attempt to impose Zoroastrianism provoked the battle of Avarayr: a military defeat for Vardan Mamikonian but a moral victory — the treaty of Nvarsak (484) guaranteed the Armenians' religious freedom. In 591 the frontier moved east in Byzantium's favour.

This same Chosroes II, whom Maurice had restored to his throne in exchange for these lands, took revenge on his benefactor: in 602 Emperor Maurice was overthrown and executed by the usurper Phocas — Chosroes II invoked this murder as a casus belli, took the Byzantine fortress of Dara after a long siege in 604, and recaptured Dvin, barely restored in 591: Armenia then served as a battlefield and logistical base for Emperor Heraclius's campaigns across the Caucasus (624-625). The peace of 628, after Chosroes II's fall, brought Armenia back under Byzantine influence — a fragile, short-lived reunification, swept away by 640 with the first Arab raids.
