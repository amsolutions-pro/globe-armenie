Bagratid Kingdoms

884 – 1045
Bagratid dynasty and sister kingdoms

In 884-885 (depending on the source), Ashot I Bagratuni was crowned "king of the Armenians", recognised by both caliph and Byzantine emperor. His grandson Ashot II "the Iron" received from Abbasid caliph al-Muqtadir, around 914-922, the higher title of "king of kings" (Shahanshah), in reward for his victories over local emirs. Ani became the capital in 961: the "city of 1001 churches", a Silk Road stage of perhaps 100,000 inhabitants. In Constantinople, Emperor Nikephoros II Phokas (963-969) annexed the Armenian kingdom of Taron around 966-968; his successor John I Tzimiskes (969-976), from the Armenian Kourkouas family (Basil I's own Armenian origin, founder of the Macedonian dynasty, is a separate and disputed historiographical question) — then conducted his Syrian campaigns with the support of the Bagratids of Ashot III.

The kingdom split into sister realms — Vaspurakan (908), Kars (963), Lori (978), Syunik (987) — easy prey: in 1021, King Senekerim Artsruni of Vaspurakan, threatened by the first Turkish raids, ceded his kingdom to Byzantine emperor Basil II in exchange for lands in Cappadocia — a negotiated cession, not a conquest, occurring just as Basil II campaigned in the Caucasus against Georgia (1021-1022). Byzantium then annexed Ani (1045) through a far murkier mechanism: Emperor Constantine IX lured the last king Gagik II to Constantinople on the pretext of negotiations, then detained him there until he surrendered his capital in exchange for lands in Cappadocia — dispossession disguised as an honorific compensation, without even a prior military defeat; the Seljuks sacked Ani in 1064 and crushed Byzantium at Manzikert (1071), opening Anatolia to the Turks.
