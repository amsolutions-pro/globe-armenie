Cilicia and the Zakarids

1080 – 1375
Armenian Kingdom of Cilicia · Zakarid principality · Mongols

Fleeing the Seljuk invasions that followed the fall of Ani (1064) and the Byzantine defeat at Manzikert (1071), Armenian princes settled in Cilicia on the Mediterranean: the Rubenid principality (1080) became a kingdom when Leo I the Magnificent was crowned in 1198. Allied to the Crusaders and then to the Mongols, the state prospered through its ports — Ayas, described by Marco Polo.

On the highlands, the Zakarian brothers, generals of Queen Tamar of Georgia, liberated the north (1199–1236) before the Mongol wave. Hetoum I submitted to the Mongols as early as 1246 and sealed the alliance with his journey to Karakorum (1254); but in 1266 the Mamluks under Baibars invaded Cilicia, sacking Sis and Adana and killing in battle Prince Toros, Hetoum I's eldest son, at the battle of Mari, where his younger brother Leo (the future Leo II) was captured and later released — the kingdom's isolation deepened with the fall of Antioch, its last crusader ally, in 1268. The decline of the Mongol Ilkhanate after 1335 left Cilicia without its protector: the strategic port of Ayas was lost as early as 1347, and Sis, the shrunken capital, fell to the Mamluks in 1375 after a month-long siege. Captured, the last king Leo V of Lusignan was imprisoned in Cairo, then freed in 1382 through Castilian-Aragonese mediation; he wandered Europe before dying in exile in Paris in 1393 — his tomb is at Saint-Denis. With the fall of Sis the last independent Armenian state died out: none would rise again until 1918.
