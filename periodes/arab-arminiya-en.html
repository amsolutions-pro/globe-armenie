Arab Arminiya

640 – 884
Umayyad then Abbasid caliphate (ostikanate)

The first Arab raids reached Dvin as early as 640. Around 652, prince Theodoros Rshtuni negotiated an autonomous tributary status for Armenia with Mu'awiya — a more structuring agreement than the initial raids, though Armeno-Arab allegiance then shifted back and forth several times before the caliphate organised the province of Arminiya around 700 — Armenia, Georgian Iberia and Caucasian Albania — governed by an ostikan from Dvin, then Bardaa. In 705, to break the nobility, the Arabs, according to tradition, burned several hundred nakharars alive in the churches of Nakhichevan and Khram.

The nakharar revolts (703, 748, 774–775 — crushed at Bagrevand — then 851-852, the direct prelude to Bugha's crackdown) bled the old Mamikonian and Kamsarakan houses, weakened militarily and demographically — to the benefit of the Bagratids, who had stayed clear of these uprisings and reaped the political dividend: Ashot Msaker received the caliphate's title of "prince of Armenia" (ishkhan) around 804-806. The Bagratid path was not straightforward, though: between 852 and 855, Abbasid general Bugha al-Kabir led a fresh crackdown, deporting several Armenian princes, including Bagratids, and the catholicos himself, to Samarra. The dynasty recovered: its descendants secured the higher title of "prince of princes" before reclaiming the crown in 884-885 depending on the source (caliphal recognition usually dated to 885).
