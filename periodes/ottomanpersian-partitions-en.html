Ottoman–Persian partitions

1514 – 1828
Ottoman Empire (west) · Safavid then Qajar Persia (east)

After the battle of Chaldiran (1514, under Selim I) Ottomans and Safavids fought over the plateau. Suleiman the Magnificent and Safavid shah Tahmasp I sealed the peace of Amasya (1555), which drew a first lasting border — Erzurum and the west to the Ottomans, Erivan and the east to the Persians — a border repeatedly upended in the interval — notably by Shah Abbas I's campaigns, which retook Erivan (1604) and then much of the Caucasus from the Ottomans (up to 1618) — before the peace of Zuhab (1639) restored a line close to that of 1555, 84 years later. In 1604 Shah Abbas deported hundreds of thousands of Armenians to Isfahan — New Julfa, which would dominate the silk trade as far as India.

After a fresh Ottoman occupation of the Caucasus (1724), Nader Shah retook Erivan in 1735 and restored Persian sovereignty; his heavy war taxation, to fund his campaigns as far as India (1739), weighed heavily on the Armenian provinces. His assassination in 1747 fragmented Persia: Caucasus governors became near-independent khans — the khanate of Erivan consolidated then, while Panah Ali Khan, founder of the Karabakh khanate, built the fortress of Shusha around 1751-1752 as his new capital — his son Ibrahim Khalil Khan succeeded him around 1759-1763 (exact date debated among sources).

The Russo-Persian War of 1826-1828, triggered by Shah Fath Ali Shah seeking to recover territories lost in 1813, ended in Persian defeat: the treaty of Turkmenchay (10 February 1828), negotiated on the Russian side by General Ivan Paskevich with diplomatic support from secretary-playwright Alexander Griboyedov — killed the following year in Tehran —, ceded the khanates of Erivan and Nakhichevan to Russia. On 21 March 1828, an imperial decree merged them into the « Armenian Oblast », the first modern territory to officially bear the name "Armenia" under Russian sovereignty; the treaty also allowed Armenians from Persia to emigrate, some 40,000 to 45,000 of whom settled in Eastern Armenia. The territorial matrix of modern Armenia, from which would come the First Republic (1918) and independent Armenia (1991).
