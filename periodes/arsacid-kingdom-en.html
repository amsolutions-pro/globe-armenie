Arsacid Kingdom

AD 66 – 428
Arsacid dynasty, between Rome and Persia

In AD 66 Tiridates I, brother of the Parthian king, received the crown of Armenia from Nero in Rome: a founding compromise — Parthian dynasty, Roman investiture.

In 114, Emperor Trajan invaded Armenia, deposed King Parthamasiris and annexed it as a Roman province — the only time in its history — just as the Roman Empire reached its greatest territorial extent (116). His successor Hadrian abandoned the province by 117-118 and restored a client kingdom.

In 224, the Sasanian Ardashir I overthrew the Parthian Arsacids: Arsacid Armenia, the dynasty's sole surviving branch, became a prime target of Sasanian hostility. Around 252, King Khosrov I was assassinated by the Parthian noble Anak on the orders of the Sasanian Shapur I, who then imposed decades of Persian domination — an episode that, according to Agathangelos, founded the family vendetta that would later drive his son Tiridates III to reclaim the throne. Anak's entire family was exterminated in reprisal, except his infant son Gregory, raised Christian in exile; back in Armenia, this son of the regicide was unmasked and thrown into the pit of Khor Virap, where he survived thirteen years, before Tiridates III, struck by madness, was healed by him and converted. In 301, this same Tiridates III, converted by St Gregory the Illuminator — who became the first catholicos, founder of a lineage that would lead the Church for a century —, made Christianity the state religion: a world first. In 405 the monk Mesrop Mashtots created the Armenian alphabet, still in use. In 387, Rome and Sasanian Persia partitioned the kingdom — about a fifth in the west for Rome, the rest under Persian sway. The monarchy was abolished by Persia in 428, at the nakharars' own request.
